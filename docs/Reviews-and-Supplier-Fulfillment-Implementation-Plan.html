1. Executive summary & sequencing

Two independent features, both signed off by Jonny in principle. They share no schema, no surface, and no critical path — so they can be built in parallel or sequenced. My recommendation:

OrderFeatureWhy this order
Phase 1a Supplier-Managed Fulfillment (AdBlue Bulk) Unblocks revenue that’s currently “coming soon” on the storefront. Bigger commercial outcome and the architectural decisions in the freight engine are time-sensitive (cheaper to build correctly now than retrofit).
Phase 1b Customer Reviews (collection + display) Smaller blast radius, compounding SEO benefit. Starts the moat clock — every week of collection is a week competitors can’t copy. Ships in parallel with 1a once the existing review infrastructure is audited.
Phase 2 Pack Existing & Pack New IBC AdBlue (rate sheets only) Pure configuration, no code, once Phase 1a’s freight engine is in place. Re-introduces forklift questions for IBC.
Phase 2 Reviews — admin reply-to-review & advanced moderation Nice-to-have once the collection flow has reviews to reply to.
Non-negotiable across both features: zero impact on existing products, existing orders, the existing MacShip flow, and the existing Stripe wiring. Both new paths sit alongside what’s already shipped, gated by clear flags (warehouses.is_supplier_managed for fulfillment, products.reviews_enabled for reviews).

2. Feature A — Customer Reviews

2.1 Why we’re building it — the four levers

Reviews aren’t a vanity feature. On a B2B catalogue site they touch four distinct levers at once:

  1. Star ratings in Google search results. Pages with valid AggregateRating + real reviews render with stars, review count, and price. Industry data: 20–35% CTR uplift at the same ranking position.
  2. Citations from AI search engines. ChatGPT, Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot, Claude, and Google AI Overviews all increasingly favour pages with real reviews when answering “where to buy {chemical}” queries.
  3. Closing the trust gap on first visits. A handful of named verified-buyer reviews from a buyer’s own state turns a curious visitor into an enquiry without them needing to call.
  4. A defensible moat. Reviews accumulate on Chem Connect’s domain. Competitors can copy the catalogue, the pricing, the design — but not the review base. Every approved review widens the gap.

2.2 Locked scope — what I’m building

The original proposal plus the eight must-add items from the scope addendum. Fully locked.

1 Post-delivery email trigger via Mailgun

Fires when an order is marked Delivered, not at checkout. Two-touch cadence:

  • +7 days post-delivery: initial review request.
  • +14 days post-delivery: single reminder if no submission.
  • No third email. (Two-touch is the B2B post-purchase industry standard.)

Reuses the existing scheduled-campaign / GHL-marketing plumbing. The trigger event is the order status transitioning to Delivered.

2 One-click magic link with signed token

Token rules, locked:

  • Expiry: 60 days from send. Token dies after that.
  • One-use only: token invalidates the moment a review is submitted against it.
  • Status gate: token only works if the underlying order is Delivered — not Shipped, not Ordered.
  • Rate limit: max 3 submission attempts per token per hour.

Buyer lands on /reviews/submit?token=XYZ with a pre-filled form (name, product, order ID). No login required. Submits star rating + headline + body + optional photos.

3 Photo upload on the submit form

Optional, max 3 images, max 5MB each. Stored alongside the review. Displayed below the review text on the product page with a lightbox click-through. Photos are a high-value visual proof signal and disproportionately weighted by AI search engines when citing.

4 Admin moderation queue at /admin/marketing/reviews

Pending reviews land in a queue. Admin sees reviewer name + verified-buyer badge, rating, body, photos, product, order ID, submission timestamp. Two actions:

  • Approve → goes live on the product page.
  • Reject → stays in DB for audit, never displayed.

No edit capability. Compliance with AU Consumer Law s18 — what the buyer wrote is what gets published.

5 Predefined rejection-reason taxonomy

Rejection forces the moderator to pick from a closed list:

  • PII / personal info
  • Libel or defamatory
  • Off-topic
  • Suspected fake
  • Profanity
  • Other (free text required)

This is the single biggest piece of evidence that rejections aren’t rating-based. If the ACCC ever queries the moderation log, the taxonomy is what proves Chem Connect wasn’t gating low ratings.

6 Manual review entry in the admin panel

For reviews that come in via phone, text, or email during the seed phase. Each entry must be tied to a real order ID and tagged as “Verified buyer (manually entered)”. Same approve / reject queue, same rejection taxonomy, same audit trail.

Without this, product pages sit at “No reviews yet” for the 6+ months it takes the magic-link flow to bootstrap.

7 Per-product “reviews disabled” flag

A boolean (products.reviews_enabled, default true) on the product admin page that prevents the post-delivery email firing for that SKU. Needed for custom blends, one-off bulk orders, and any product where a public review wouldn’t make sense.

8 Privacy Act review-removal endpoint

If a customer requests removal under the Privacy Act 1988, admin gets a one-click hard-delete action that:

  • Wipes the review record
  • Wipes the underlying token
  • Logs the deletion (moderator + timestamp + reason “Privacy Act request”) in a separate review_audit_log table

Hard-delete, not soft-delete — the audit log is the proof the deletion happened.

9 Minimum-threshold gate on AggregateRating schema

The aggregateRating JSON-LD block does not emit until a product has at least 3 approved reviews. Below that, the page renders with no schema and the empty-state message (“No reviews yet — be the first”). Reasons:

  • One 1-star review pulls the AggregateRating to 1.0 across the entire product.
  • Google generally won’t render the star result below 3 reviews anyway.

2.3 The collection flow

Customer places + receives an order | | (status transitions to Delivered) v Wait 7 days (configurable via the existing scheduled-campaign engine) | v Mailgun sends the initial review request email | | +14 days (if no submission yet) v Mailgun sends the single reminder | | (one-click magic link, signed token, 60-day expiry) v .../reviews/submit?token=XYZ -- prefilled form, optional photos | | (status: pending; token consumed; rate-limit checked) v Admin moderation queue at /admin/marketing/reviews | | Approve --> publishes on product page | Reject --> taxonomy reason recorded, stays in DB, no display v Star rating + review block live on the product page + AggregateRating + Review[] in JSON-LD (gated at >=3 approved reviews)

2.4 What the buyer sees on the product page

Aggregate rating

Star average + total review count next to the product title. Only visible (and only schema-emitted) once the product has ≥ 3 approved reviews.

Individual review cards

Star rating, headline, body, reviewer name + city + state, date, “Verified buyer” badge, optional photos with lightbox.

Empty-state fallback

If a product has fewer than 3 approved reviews, the section says “No reviews yet — be the first”. No broken UI, no fake placeholder.

2.5 Compliance posture

RuleHow this implementation handles it
Reviews must be from genuine buyers (Google policy + AU Consumer Law) Magic-link tokens are tied to real order IDs and signed. A review can’t be submitted without a valid token. Manual seed entries are tied to real order IDs and clearly tagged.
No editing reviews (s18, AU Consumer Law) Admin can only approve or reject — no edit capability anywhere in the moderation UI.
No “review gating” (asking only happy customers) Every delivered order with reviews_enabled = true triggers a request. No pre-screening for satisfaction.
Negative reviews must not be hidden Rejection only allowed for predefined policy violations (taxonomy locked). Low-rating-only rejections are not possible — the dropdown doesn’t offer that as a reason, and the audit log proves it.
Audit trail for moderation Every approve / reject / hard-delete records moderator, timestamp, action, and (where applicable) reason. Immutable log table.
Privacy Act 1988 removal Hard-delete endpoint with audit-log entry — covered in scope item 8.

2.6 Schema.org wiring

The product detail page emits a Schema.org Product JSON-LD block. Once a product has ≥ 3 approved reviews, the implementation splices two extra fields into that block:

AggregateRating

Drives the star result in Google search. Average rating, review count, best/worst rating bounds.

Review[] array

Up to 10 reviews per product (newest-first). Author, rating, date, body. Picked up by ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews when those crawl the page.

2.7 Data model — Reviews

-- Per-product flag, default ON
alter table products
  add column if not exists reviews_enabled boolean not null default true;

-- The review submission tokens (one per delivered order line)
create table review_tokens (
  id uuid primary key default uuid_generate_v4(),
  order_id uuid not null references orders on delete cascade,
  product_id uuid not null references products on delete cascade,
  signed_token text not null unique,
  sent_at timestamptz not null default now(),
  expires_at timestamptz not null,           -- sent_at + 60 days
  consumed_at timestamptz,                   -- set when a review submits
  attempt_count integer not null default 0,  -- rate-limit counter (per hour)
  last_attempt_at timestamptz
);

-- Reviews themselves
create table reviews (
  id uuid primary key default uuid_generate_v4(),
  product_id uuid not null references products on delete cascade,
  order_id uuid references orders on delete set null,
  token_id uuid references review_tokens on delete set null,
  source text not null check (source in ('magic_link','manual')),
  reviewer_display_name text not null,        -- format chosen at submit time, see 2.8
  reviewer_city text,
  reviewer_state text,
  rating integer not null check (rating between 1 and 5),
  headline text not null,
  body text not null,
  consent_given boolean not null,
  status text not null default 'pending'
    check (status in ('pending','approved','rejected','removed')),
  rejection_reason text                       -- enum-checked at app layer
    check (rejection_reason in (
      'pii','libel','off_topic','suspected_fake','profanity','other'
    ) or rejection_reason is null),
  rejection_notes text,                       -- free text when 'other'
  submitted_at timestamptz not null default now(),
  moderated_at timestamptz,
  moderated_by uuid references users
);

create table review_photos (
  id uuid primary key default uuid_generate_v4(),
  review_id uuid not null references reviews on delete cascade,
  storage_path text not null,
  position smallint not null check (position between 1 and 3)
);

-- Immutable audit log
create table review_audit_log (
  id uuid primary key default uuid_generate_v4(),
  review_id uuid references reviews on delete set null,
  action text not null check (action in (
    'submitted','approved','rejected','hard_deleted','reply_added'
  )),
  actor_id uuid references users,
  reason text,
  detail jsonb,
  created_at timestamptz not null default now()
);

3. Feature B — Supplier-Managed Fulfillment

3.1 Why we’re building it

AdBlue is currently “coming soon” on the storefront because the existing fulfillment pipeline (Stripe → MacShip → CC warehouse → MacShip tracking) doesn’t fit a supplier-fulfilled, tanker pump-in product. This feature adds a second fulfillment path so:

  • External suppliers can be onboarded as a special kind of warehouse, with their own freight rules and their own login.
  • The freight engine quotes from the supplier’s distance-bracketed rate sheet rather than calling MacShip.
  • The supplier sees only their orders, can update ETA and dispatch state, and the buyer is notified through Chem Connect.
  • Adding the next supplier becomes a configuration job, not a development job.

All eight understanding rows in section 2 of the requirements doc are confirmed by Jonny. The architecture lands cleanly on the existing warehouses, product_warehouses, and warehouse_product_pricing tables.

3.3 Locked scope — components I’m building

Every component below is locked. Each maps to one or more migrations in 3.6 and one or more workstreams in 3.12. Same format as the Reviews scope (2.2) so the build surface is comparable.

1 Supplier-managed warehouse type

An existing warehouses row can be flagged as supplier-managed via is_supplier_managed = true. This single flag drives every downstream behaviour change — freight strategy, PO routing, dashboard scoping, buyer order-page surface. Existing warehouses are unaffected (default is false).

Created from an admin form that captures: warehouse name, address (origin postcode for distance lookups), one or more supplier users (component 2), and the rate-sheet links (component 3).

2 Multi-user supplier model

N users per supplier-managed warehouse via the warehouse_users junction table. Each user has role flags:

  • receives_po_emails — whether this user gets the purchase-order email when an order arrives
  • can_update_orders — whether this user can edit dispatch state from the dashboard
  • is_primary_contact — flagged for admin reference

AdBlue Phase 1 = one user, all flags true (Jonny confirmed). Architecture handles future suppliers with separate sales/dispatch staff without code changes.

3 Freight engine — rate sheets + brackets

Each supplier-managed warehouse has one or more rate sheets. Each rate sheet declares its unit_type (per_litre, flat_per_consignment, per_kg, per_pallet, per_zone) and is composed of distance brackets (0–100km, 101–200km, … up to 4000+km) with a rate per bracket.

Phase 1 wires a single rate sheet: AdBlue Bulk Post-14Jul25 with unit_type = per_litre. Pack Existing and Pack New rate sheets are loaded as configuration (no code) in Phase 2.

4 Product-to-rate-sheet mapping

The product_freight_rate_sheets table maps each product + packaging size combination to a rate sheet. packaging_size_id NULL means “applies to all sizes for this product”. Phase 1 wires AdBlue Bulk to the AdBlue Bulk Post-14Jul25 sheet; other AdBlue packaging sizes get their mappings added in Phase 2.

5 Distance + bracket lookup at checkout

Postcode → road distance via Google Distance Matrix API (not straight-line). Round up to the next 100km bracket (so 350km uses the 301–400 row). Result is cached per (origin, destination) for the order’s lifetime to control API cost.

The freight number returned to the cart = rate per unit × quantity (where applicable) × bracket lookup. It feeds lib/orders/calculate.ts the same way MacShip’s freight number does today — the rest of the order math (GST, processing fee, total) needs no changes.

6 Configurable site-access question engine

The product_checkout_questions table defines questions per product + packaging size. Each question has a question_key, label, question_type (yes_no, text, number, select), optional options (for select), required flag, and display order. Answers are stored as orders.site_access_answers jsonb.

Phase 1 ships with the seven AdBlue Bulk questions in 3.4 wired up. Phase 2 adds forklift + tank-fitting questions for IBC. Future supplier-specific questions are configuration, not code.

7 Truck-access warning at checkout

If the buyer answers “No” to the 19-metre truck access question, the checkout shows a warning but lets the order through. The locked copy:

“Our standard delivery is a 19-metre tanker. If this truck cannot access your site, the supplier will contact you to arrange an alternative, which may involve additional cost or delay.”

The “No” answer is surfaced prominently on the supplier dashboard so the supplier calls the buyer before dispatch. Hard-blocking the order would lose sales unnecessarily — almost every site has a workaround.

8 PO email to supplier on order placement

When a buyer pays for an order containing a supplier-managed product, the system sends a purchase-order email to every warehouse_users row with receives_po_emails = true. Email includes:

  • Buyer name, full delivery address, contact phone and email
  • Products and quantities
  • All site-access answers (truck access, tank capacity, fill point, hours, induction, hardstand, fittings)
  • Order ID and a deep link straight into the order detail page in the supplier dashboard

Email is Chem Connect-branded and routed through the same Mailgun config the rest of the platform uses.

9 Supplier dashboard (scoped, read-mostly)

A dedicated dashboard surface, Chem Connect-branded, scoped to the supplier’s warehouses only. The supplier sees:

  • A list of their warehouses’ orders with filters (status, date)
  • Order detail with full address, site-access answers, products and quantities
  • Editable fields: supplier_dispatch_date, estimated_delivery, supplier_dispatch_notes, supplier_tracking_url, status

The supplier cannot see other Chem Connect orders, products, or customers, cannot edit products, and cannot refund or cancel.

10 Two-date model (dispatch + ETA)

The supplier sets two dates — orders.supplier_dispatch_date (new) for when goods leave the depot, and orders.estimated_delivery (reused) for when they arrive on site. Both editable any time, both visible to the buyer, both fire a buyer notification when set or changed.

Two dates is what Jonny asked for — concrete plants run on tight chemical inventory and need dispatch and arrival as separate planning signals.

11 Optional external tracking URL

If the supplier’s carrier has its own portal, the supplier pastes a tracking URL into the order. orders.supplier_tracking_url stores it; the buyer order page surfaces it as a link if present. No tracking URL = the dispatch date + ETA + notes are the buyer’s only signal.

12 Buyer order page — supplier-fulfillment surface

For supplier-managed orders, the buyer order page replaces the MacShip scan-tracking surface with: dispatch date, ETA, dispatch notes (if any), and the optional tracking URL. Layout matches the existing MacShip surface so the buyer doesn’t feel a regression.

13 Buyer notifications on date set / change

The buyer receives an email when:

  • The supplier first sets the dispatch date
  • The supplier first sets the ETA
  • The supplier changes either date later (e.g. delay)

Each email links back to the order page. No extra emails on dispatch-notes or tracking-URL changes — those just update silently on the page.

14 Admin SLA / stuck-order flag

The admin orders page surfaces a flag for supplier-managed orders where estimated_delivery IS NULL after a configurable threshold (default 24 hours from order placement). Visible only to admins; not surfaced to the buyer. Phase 1 = flag-only. Phase 2 layers automated supplier nudge emails on top.

15 Reconciliation view (full margin per order)

An admin-only view aggregates per supplier-managed order:

  • Sale price (from order_items)
  • Product cost (from existing warehouse_product_pricing, joined on warehouse + product + packaging size)
  • Buyer-paid shipping (from order totals)
  • Supplier freight cost (from orders.supplier_freight_cost, settable by admin)
  • Full margin = sale price − product cost − supplier freight cost

Per Jonny’s clarification: freight margin alone is half a tool — the Xero entries need full margin (sale − product cost − freight) so this view drives all reconciliation in one query rather than two. warehouse_product_pricing already carries cost per warehouse + product + packaging, so this is an extension of the same query, not a new data source.

16 Variance check on dispatch

When the supplier marks an order ready to dispatch, the system recalculates freight using the supplier’s confirmed origin depot. If the recalculated number differs from the buyer-quoted number by more than a configurable threshold, the order is flagged for admin review and the buyer is notified before dispatch.

Default threshold (locked): 10% or $100, whichever is greater. Configurable later from the admin settings if usage shows the default is too tight or too loose.

17 Mixed-cart blocker (with usage counter)

Phase 1 explicitly blocks mixed carts — a single checkout cannot contain both a MacShip product and a supplier-managed product. The cart shows a clear message asking the buyer to split into two orders.

Counter (added per Jonny’s request): every time the block fires, log a row to a small mixed_cart_block_events table capturing timestamp, user (if logged in), and the SKU pair that triggered it. Admin sees a count + recent-events list on a dedicated metrics tile. The point is to size Phase 2: if it fires twice a quarter we leave it; if it fires twice a week we prioritise unbundling.

18 Phase-1 storefront visibility for AdBlue

The seeded AdBlue product currently has four packaging sizes: 10L Jerry Can, 200L Drum, 1000L IBC, and Bulk. Phase 1 only wires the Bulk freight path. To avoid a half-broken UX where the buyer can pick a packaging size with no working freight quote, the other three sizes are hidden from the public product page in Phase 1.

Implementation: a per-packaging-size is_visible_on_storefront boolean flag (default true), set to false for the three non-Bulk AdBlue sizes during the Phase 1 deployment. Phase 2 flips them back on once the Pack Existing / Pack New rate sheets are loaded and forklift / tank-fitting questions are wired.

3.4 Site-access questions (configurable per product+packaging)

Locked question set for AdBlue Bulk Phase 1:

  • Can a 19-metre rigid/tanker truck access the delivery site? (yes/no)
  • Tank capacity and approximate empty space at time of delivery (numeric, litres)
  • Fill point location and approximate hose run distance from where the truck can park (text + numeric)
  • Site operating hours and after-hours contact number (text)
  • Site induction or sign-in requirement (yes/no + free text notes)
  • Hardstand suitable for a loaded tanker (yes/no)
  • Existing tank fitting type (select — options derived from supplier’s hose fittings, see 3.11)

Forklift availability dropped from Phase 1, re-introduced when Pack Existing / Pack New IBC variants ship in Phase 2.

Architectural note: these questions are configured in a product_checkout_questions table (see 3.6), not hardcoded into checkout. The same engine handles future supplier-specific or product-specific question sets without code changes.

3.5 The freight engine — the architectural centrepiece

Why this matters: Even within AdBlue alone, the freight matrix has two fundamentally different calculation models — per-litre × distance for Bulk, flat-per-consignment × distance for Pack. Other suppliers will likely add per-pallet, weight-based, and zone-based pricing. A single-column lookup is not enough, and retrofitting that later is a rebuild.

The freight engine is built around three primitives:

PrimitiveRole
supplier_rate_sheets Each rate sheet declares its unit_type (per_litre, flat_per_consignment, per_kg, per_pallet, etc.), its origin postcode, and active status.
supplier_rate_sheet_brackets Distance brackets (e.g. 0–100km, 101–200km, …) and the rate per bracket.
product_freight_rate_sheets Maps a product + packaging size combination to a rate sheet. packaging_size_id NULL = applies to all sizes for that product.

Rate sheetUnit typePhase
AdBlue Bulk Post-14Jul25per_litrePhase 1
AdBlue Pack Existing Post-14Jul25flat_per_consignmentPhase 2 (config only)
AdBlue Pack New Post-14Jul25flat_per_consignmentPhase 2 (config only)

Where it plugs into existing code

The cleanest place for the strategy split is lib/macship/warehouse-selector.ts, which becomes a more general fulfillment router:

  • If the selected warehouse has is_supplier_managed = false → current MacShip path runs unchanged.
  • If is_supplier_managed = true → new SupplierManagedStrategy is invoked, which looks up the rate sheet for each cart line, computes the freight cost, and skips the MacShip quote call entirely.

lib/orders/calculate.ts already calls a warehouse selector and handles the freight number cleanly. The supplier-managed path returns a freight number the same way MacShip does — the rest of the order math (GST, processing fee, total) works without changes.

  rate_sheet_id uuid not null references supplier_rate_sheets on delete cascade,
  distance_from_km integer not null,
  distance_to_km integer not null,
  rate numeric(10, 4) not null
);

create table product_freight_rate_sheets (
  product_id uuid not null references products on delete cascade,
  packaging_size_id uuid references packaging_sizes on delete cascade,
  rate_sheet_id uuid not null references supplier_rate_sheets on delete cascade,
  primary key (product_id, packaging_size_id)
);

-- 3.6.5 Configurable site-access questions
create table product_checkout_questions (
  id uuid primary key default uuid_generate_v4(),
  product_id uuid not null references products on delete cascade,
  packaging_size_id uuid references packaging_sizes on delete cascade,
  question_key text not null,                    -- 'truck_access_19m', 'tank_capacity', etc.
  label text not null,
  question_type text not null check (question_type in (
    'yes_no','text','number','select'
  )),
  options jsonb,                                  -- for 'select' type
  required boolean not null default false,
  display_order smallint not null default 0
);

-- 3.6.6 New columns on orders for the supplier-managed flow
alter table orders
  add column if not exists supplier_dispatch_date date,
  add column if not exists supplier_dispatch_notes text,
  add column if not exists supplier_tracking_url text,
  add column if not exists supplier_freight_cost numeric(10, 2),
  add column if not exists site_access_answers jsonb;

-- 3.6.7 Multi-user supplier model (N users per supplier-managed warehouse)
-- Junction table so a supplier-managed warehouse can have one user
-- (single-person supplier) or several (e.g. separate sales / dispatch).
-- Role flags decide who receives PO emails and who can update orders.
create table warehouse_users (
  id uuid primary key default uuid_generate_v4(),
  warehouse_id uuid not null references warehouses on delete cascade,
  user_id uuid not null references users on delete cascade,
  receives_po_emails boolean not null default true,
  can_update_orders boolean not null default true,
  is_primary_contact boolean not null default false,
  created_at timestamptz not null default now(),
  unique (warehouse_id, user_id)
);
-- The supplier dashboard scopes each user to the warehouses listed in
-- this table; PO emails are sent to all users with receives_po_emails = true.

3.9 What the three user groups see

Each side of the platform gets a distinct surface. None of them overlap, and the supplier deliberately sees the smallest slice.

Buyer

Same Chem Connect storefront as today. The visible differences when ordering AdBlue (Bulk):

  • Extra site-access questions on checkout (component 6)
  • Truck-access warning if they answer “No” (component 7)
  • Cart blocks if they mix supplier-managed + MacShip products in one checkout (component 17)
  • Order page shows supplier dispatch date + ETA + notes + optional tracking URL instead of MacShip scan tracking (component 12)
  • Email notifications when the supplier sets / changes dispatch date or ETA (component 13)

Admin (Jonny)

Sees everything — including supplier orders — from the master admin. Net new admin work this feature adds:

  • Supplier-managed warehouse creation form (components 1, 2)
  • Rate-sheet + bracket configuration UI (components 3, 4)
  • Site-access question configuration UI (component 6)
  • Per-warehouse supplier user management (component 2)
  • SLA stuck-order flag in the orders list (component 14)
  • Reconciliation view: buyer-paid shipping vs supplier_freight_cost margin (component 15)
  • Variance-check review surface (component 16)

Supplier

A focused, scoped, read-mostly dashboard. Sees only their warehouse’s orders. They can:

  • Browse + filter their orders
  • Open an order and see address, contact details, site-access answers, products, quantities
  • Set / update supplier_dispatch_date, estimated_delivery, supplier_dispatch_notes, supplier_tracking_url
  • Mark order dispatched / delivered

They cannot see other warehouses, edit products, refund, or cancel.

Multi-user note: a supplier-managed warehouse can have N users (sales / dispatch / ops) via the warehouse_users junction table. Each user’s role flags decide whether they receive PO emails (receives_po_emails) and whether they can update orders (can_update_orders). For AdBlue Phase 1, Jonny confirmed it’s one person handling both PO receipt and dispatch updates — so one warehouse_users row with all flags true. The junction table is in place from day 1 so future suppliers with separate sales/dispatch staff are a configuration job, not a code change.

3.10 Compliance & operational safeguards

  • Buyer pays Chem Connect. No marketplace splits, no separate Stripe accounts. Reconciliation in Xero, same as today.
  • Supplier sees buyer contact details. Contractual safeguard: clause in the supplier agreement preventing direct-sales bypass.
  • No refunds, no product edits, no cancellations from the supplier dashboard. Refund / cancel actions stay with admin.
  • Freight quote vs final cost variance. If the supplier’s recalculated freight differs from the indicative quote by more than a configurable threshold, flag the order and notify the buyer before dispatch.
  • Audit trail on supplier dashboard actions. Every change to dispatch_date / ETA / status records actor + timestamp.

7. Risks & mitigations

RiskImpactMitigation
Existing review infrastructure isn’t as built as the original proposal implies Reviews build estimate balloons; “glue work” becomes “greenfield work” Audit upfront (M1). Surface as explicit additional scope before locking hours.
Distance calculation API choice has cost / accuracy tradeoffs Either inaccurate freight (lost margin) or expensive per-quote API calls Use Google Distance Matrix, cache results per (origin, destination) for the order’s lifetime, and round up to bracket boundaries (which absorbs minor inaccuracy).
Supplier doesn’t respond to PO emails reliably Orders sit in limbo, buyer trust erodes SLA stuck-order flag in admin (component 14 / 3.7 item B). Phase 2 adds automated supplier nudges.
Mixed-cart orders (one MacShip product + one supplier product) Two freight numbers, two POs, confusing checkout Phase 1 decision: block mixed carts at checkout with a clear message. Phase 2 (post-launch) revisit if the use case actually emerges.
Supplier sees buyer contact details and uses them for direct sales Lost margin, lost trust with buyers Contractual safeguard in supplier agreement (per Q8). No technical masking.
One-star “troll” review tanks AggregateRating early in a product’s life SERP CTR drop from a volatile rating Threshold gate (≥ 3 approved reviews) on JSON-LD. Manual seed entry to bridge the gap.
Magic-link token leaks via email forwarding Fraudulent reviews under a real customer’s name One-use, status-gated, 60-day expiry, 3 attempts/hour rate limit. All four enforced server-side.
ACCC scrutiny on moderation decisions Regulator finds rejection patterns are rating-correlated Closed rejection-reason taxonomy. Immutable audit log. Approve-or-reject only — no edit. The audit log is the defence.
