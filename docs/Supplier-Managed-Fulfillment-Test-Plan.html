Supplier-Managed Fulfillment - End-to-End Flow

A plain-English walkthrough of how Chem Connect's supplier-managed fulfillment works, from setting up a supplier to placing buyer orders to paying the supplier. This document is for everyone - admin, supplier, finance, and anyone testing the system.

Who's involved? Three parties exchange money and information in this flow:
  • Buyer - the customer placing an order on Chem Connect.
  • CQVS / Chem Connect - the marketplace operator. Earns a margin on every order.
  • Supplier - the warehouse / carrier that holds the product and arranges delivery.
  • Admin - CQVS staff member operating the back office (onboards suppliers, reviews variance claims, reconciles billing).

1. The big picture

Some products on Chem Connect are supplier-managed. This means the supplier (not CQVS, not MacShip) is responsible for holding stock and arranging delivery. Today this is used for AdBlue Bulk Tanker, but the system is generic - any product can be flagged supplier-managed.

Compared to the standard MacShip flow, the supplier-managed flow has these key differences:

StepStandard (MacShip) flowSupplier-managed flow
StockHeld by CQVS at known warehousesHeld by the supplier at their depot
Freight quote at checkoutMacShip API live-quoteCalculated from the supplier's own freight matrix
CarrierMacShip-booked carrierSupplier arranges (truck, pickup, scheduling)
TrackingMacShip consignment ID + carrier linkSupplier-provided tracking link (optional)
Who CQVS paysMacShip + product manufacturerThe supplier directly (single PO)

The customer experience is intentionally identical. The buyer doesn't need to know which fulfillment path applies - they just see a freight quote, place an order, and get notifications.

2. Onboarding a new supplier Admin

Admin sets up a supplier in three layers: the warehouse record, the supplier user account, and the product mappings.

2.1 Create / flag a supplier-managed warehouse

  1. Go to Admin → Warehouses and either create a new warehouse or open an existing one.
  2. Fill in the warehouse address (this is the origin for freight distance calculation).
  3. Tick "This is a supplier-managed warehouse".
  4. Set the contact email on the warehouse - this is the address Xero sends Purchase Orders to. If this is missing, the supplier won't get the auto-emailed PO.
  5. Go to the Xero Contact ID field and either link an existing Xero contact or have admin create one in Xero first, then paste the ID here.
Why this matters: if the warehouse doesn't have a Xero Contact ID and a contact email, the system can still take buyer orders, but the supplier won't receive a Xero PO. Admin sees a clear error in the Xero integration log.

2.2 Add a supplier user (the human who logs in)

  1. From Admin → Supplier Fulfillment → Warehouse → Users, click Invite supplier user.
  2. Enter the supplier's email and tick the permissions:
    • Receives PO emails - gets notified inside the platform every time a buyer places an order.
    • Can update orders - can confirm dispatch, set ETA, edit the freight matrix, raise variance claims. Read-only suppliers see orders but can't change them.
  3. Click Send invite. The supplier receives a Chem Connect-branded invite email with a sign-up link.
Buyer / supplier separation: the system rejects the invite if the email already has buyer history (any past order placed by that email). Suppliers and buyers are strictly separate accounts in Chem Connect. If admin needs to give an existing buyer supplier access, the supplier needs to use a different email.

2.3 Map products to this warehouse

For each product the supplier carries:

  1. Open the warehouse settings.
  2. Go to Warehouse Pricing and enter the cost price per packaging size (this is what CQVS will pay the supplier for the product, not what the buyer sees).
  3. Optionally add per-product checkout questions (e.g. "is there a forklift on site?", "tank fitting type?").

2.4 Set up the freight rate sheets

The supplier's freight cost is calculated from a distance-based rate matrix. Admin can seed this initially, but maintaining it is the supplier's responsibility going forward (see §3).

Each rate sheet has:

  • Name - e.g. "AdBlue Bulk", "Pack Existing", "Pack New"
  • Unit type - one of: per litre, flat per consignment, per kg, per pallet, per zone
  • Origin postcode - defaults to the warehouse postcode unless overridden
  • Distance brackets - e.g. 0-100 km @ $0.07/L, 101-200 km @ $0.08/L, etc.

Admin then maps each (product, packaging size) to one of the rate sheets so the system knows which sheet to use when a buyer orders that combination.

3. Supplier sets up their freight matrix Supplier

After the supplier accepts the invite and signs in, they land on the Supplier Portal. Two main pages: Orders and Freight Matrix.

3.1 The Freight Matrix page

The supplier's freight matrix is theirs to maintain. They can:

  • View all rate sheets attached to their warehouse(s).
  • Edit any bracket's rate inline.
  • Add new brackets or remove old ones.
  • Create a brand-new rate sheet (e.g. for a new product line).
  • Delete a rate sheet.
  • Toggle a sheet's "Active" flag (deactivated sheets aren't used for new quotes).

3.2 Bulk upload (CSV)

For large rate updates, suppliers can:

  1. Click Download blank template on the Freight Matrix page - a CSV opens with the right column headers.
  2. Fill in their distance brackets and rates in Excel / Google Sheets.
  3. Save and upload back. The system parses it, shows a preview, and lets the supplier tick which columns to import.
  4. Click Import. All rows are created in one batch (or rolled back if anything fails).
Important: updates to the freight matrix only affect future orders. Already-placed orders are locked to the rate that was in effect at the moment the buyer paid. So a supplier raising their rates today doesn't retroactively change what they're paid on yesterday's orders.

4. Buyer places an order Buyer

4.1 Browsing and adding to cart

  1. Buyer opens the Chem Connect storefront, browses products, and adds an AdBlue Bulk Tanker (for example) to their cart.
  2. For bulk-tanker style products, the buyer enters the number of litres they want (presets at 1,000 / 5,000 / 10,000 / 20,000 are offered).
  3. They proceed to checkout.

4.2 Site-access answers

Supplier-managed products often need site-access information so the supplier can plan delivery. The buyer answers these per-product questions on the checkout page (e.g. "is there hardstand?", "tank fitting type?", "site induction required?"). Answers are stored on the order and visible to both supplier and admin.

4.3 Freight quote

When the buyer enters a delivery address, the system:

  1. Identifies that this cart contains a supplier-managed product.
  2. Looks up which rate sheet applies (for AdBlue Bulk Tanker - the "Bulk" rate sheet).
  3. Calculates the road distance from the supplier's origin postcode to the delivery postcode.
  4. Rounds up to the next 100 km bracket and finds the matching row.
  5. Applies the formula:
    • Per litre: rate × litres × quantity
    • Flat per consignment: rate × quantity
    • Per kg / pallet: rate × units × quantity
  6. Adds any minimum charge if applicable.

The freight is shown on the checkout page as "Supplier freight quote" with a note: "Final freight is reconfirmed by the supplier on dispatch. You'll be notified if it differs from this quote."

4.4 Payment

The buyer pays via Stripe (credit card) or via Purchase Order. Once paid (or once the PO is approved by admin), the order is finalized and a number of things happen automatically:

  1. The order's freight quote is locked. Even if the supplier later changes the rate matrix, this order stays at the price the buyer was shown.
  2. The full breakdown of the freight calculation (which rate sheet, which bracket, what distance, what rate) is saved on the order as an audit trail.
  3. A Xero Purchase Order is created on the supplier's Xero contact (line items at cost price, plus a freight line equal to the locked freight). Xero auto-emails the PO to the supplier's contact email.
  4. An in-platform PO email is also sent to the supplier user(s) with the order details, site-access answers, and a deep link to the supplier portal.
  5. The order shows up immediately in the supplier's orders list.

5. Supplier confirms dispatch and delivery Supplier

The supplier opens the order in their portal and works through these fields:

  1. Dispatch date - when the truck leaves the depot. Confirming this triggers an email to the buyer.
  2. Estimated delivery date - when the buyer should expect it. Triggers another email.
  3. Tracking URL - optional link to the carrier's tracking page. Triggers an email when added (or removed/changed).
  4. Dispatch notes - free-text notes for the buyer (e.g. "driver will call ahead").
  5. Origin postcode - if dispatching from a different depot than the warehouse default.
  6. Status - moved through "Received → Processing → In Transit → Delivered".

Every change is recorded in an audit log visible to both supplier and admin (timestamped, with old / new value).

5.1 Locking the supplier's freight payout

When the supplier moves the order to In Transit, the system locks the supplier's freight payout (supplier_freight_cost) at exactly the amount that was quoted to the buyer at checkout. Default model: supplier paid quoted freight, no surprises either way.

The exception is the variance claim path - see §6.

6. Variance claim path (the contract exception) Supplier Admin

If the supplier's actual freight cost ends up higher than what their matrix said, the contract default is "supplier eats it". But the contract also allows for legitimate exceptions where the supplier can raise a claim - and admin reviews it.

6.1 When the supplier can raise a claim

Only before dispatch (while the order is in Received or Processing). Once dispatch begins, the claim window has closed - per contract, late notifications can't be honored.

6.2 The supplier's claim form

On the order detail page, the supplier sees a Freight Variance Claim card. Clicking Raise variance claim opens a form:

  • Claimed amount (in AUD)
  • Notified at - when they originally informed admin
  • Notification evidence - free-text describing how/why they notified (e.g. "emailed admin@chemconnect.com on 4 May with carrier quote")

The supplier can withdraw a pending claim before admin reviews it.

6.3 Admin reviews the claim

Admin opens Admin → Supplier Fulfillment → Variance Claims. They see all pending / approved / rejected claims with full context: order number, warehouse, quoted vs claimed amount, notification timestamp, evidence.

Admin can:

  • Approve with the full claimed amount, OR with a reduced amount (e.g. partial approval).
  • Reject, with a decision note explaining why (e.g. "matrix should have been updated in advance").

Both decisions trigger emails (see §9).

6.4 What happens after approval

When the supplier next moves the order to In Transit, the system reads the latest approved claim and sets supplier_freight_cost to the approved amount instead of the original quoted freight. The audit log records the change.

Where the variance lands: the buyer always pays the originally quoted freight (locked at checkout). If admin approves a higher amount for the supplier, the difference comes out of CQVS's product margin. CQVS effectively absorbs the variance to honor the supplier contract.

7. Xero billing and reconciliation Admin

Xero has two key documents for each supplier-managed order:

DocumentWhat it representsWhen created
Purchase Order (PO)The order commitment. Frozen, emailed to the supplier as a PDF.Automatically when the buyer pays.
Bill (ACCPAY invoice)The actual amount CQVS owes the supplier. This is what gets paid.When admin clicks "Mark as billed" in Xero on the PO.

7.1 Standard case (no variance claim)

  1. Buyer pays. Xero PO is auto-created and emailed to supplier.
  2. Supplier dispatches and delivers.
  3. Admin opens the PO in Xero, clicks Mark as billed. Xero pre-fills a draft Bill from the PO.
  4. Admin approves the Bill as-is. Pays the supplier.
  5. Admin returns to Reconciliation in CQVS and clicks Mark as billed on the row to flag the order as fully reconciled.

7.2 With an approved variance claim

Same flow but with one extra step. The PO in Xero doesn't change - that document is sacred (the supplier has the original PDF, modifying it after the fact is bad practice). Instead the variance is reflected on the Bill, which is the document admin can edit.

  1. The reconciliation page in CQVS highlights orders with approved variance claims in amber. The "Bill freight" column tells admin exactly what to enter.
  2. Admin opens the Xero PO, clicks Mark as billed.
  3. On the draft Bill, admin edits the freight line from the original quoted amount to the approved-claim amount before approving the Bill.
  4. Admin returns to CQVS and clicks Mark as billed on the row. Highlight clears.
Reconciliation tabs: the page has three filter tabs - All, Pending billing (orders with approved claims that admin still needs to bill), and Billed in Xero (already done). Each tab shows a count.

8. The money flow, end to end

Here's a worked example with real numbers to make the flow concrete.

8.1 Example: 700 L of AdBlue Bulk Tanker to Sydney 2000 (paid by card)

ItemAmountHow it's calculated
Product subtotal$805.00700 L × $1.15 (customer-facing price)
Supplier freight$49.00700 L × $0.07/L from the supplier's 0-100 km bracket
GST (10%)$85.4010% on (product + freight) = ($805 + $49) × 10%
Card processing fee$18.41Stripe fee passed through to the customer (see breakdown below)
Buyer total$957.81Locked once paid
How the card processing fee is calculated (Chem Connect passes Stripe's actual fee through to the buyer; PO orders pay $0 since they don't go through Stripe):
  1. Stripe charges 1.75% × (subtotal + freight + GST) + $0.30 fixed.
    For this order: ($805 + $49 + $85.40) × 1.75% + $0.30 = $939.40 × 1.75% + $0.30 = $16.74.
  2. 10% GST is added on top of the Stripe fee itself: $16.74 × 10% = $1.67.
  3. Card processing fee shown to the buyer: $16.74 + $1.67 = $18.41.

Effective rate is ~1.96% on this order (not a flat 3%). The $0.30 fixed component makes very small orders feel more expensive in percentage terms, and very large orders cheaper.

8.2 What CQVS pays the supplier (default - no claim)

ItemAmount
Product cost (700 × $0.80)$560.00
Freight pass-through$49.00
PO / Bill total$609.00 + GST

8.3 CQVS gross margin

ComponentAmount
Product margin (700 × ($1.15 - $0.80))$245.00
Freight margin ($49 - $49)$0.00
Total gross margin$245.00

8.4 With an approved variance claim of $65

ItemAmount
Buyer paid (unchanged)$957.81
CQVS pays supplier (Bill total)$625.00 + GST
Product margin$245.00
Freight margin ($49 - $65)-$16.00
Total gross margin$229.00

So the $16 difference (between the locked buyer freight and the approved supplier amount) comes out of CQVS's pocket. That's why approval is admin-gated - it has a real cost.

10. Testing the whole flow

This is the smoke test that exercises the full happy path plus the variance exception. Run it once after each release that touches supplier-managed code.

You'll need three accounts: an admin, a supplier user (with edit permission), and a buyer.

10.1 Onboarding sanity check

1Admin sees the supplier warehouse at Admin → Supplier Fulfillment. Warehouse has Xero Contact ID and contact_email set. Has at least one rate sheet with brackets. At least one product mapped to it with a cost price.
2Supplier signs in at the supplier portal. Sees Orders + Freight Matrix in the nav. Freight Matrix page shows their rate sheets.

10.2 Freight matrix CRUD

3Supplier edits a bracket rate, saves, refreshes - change persists. Reverts the change.
4Supplier downloads the blank CSV template, fills in 3-4 brackets, uploads, imports. New rate sheet appears in their list. Deletes it to clean up.

10.3 Order placement

5Buyer places a Stripe order for 700 L AdBlue Bulk Tanker to Sydney 2000. Sees a freight quote at checkout (e.g. $49). Pays.
6Buyer receives an order confirmation email. Order shows up in their dashboard with the supplier-managed freight breakdown panel.
7Supplier receives two emails: an in-platform PO email and a Xero PO PDF. The PO has product line at cost ($560) and a freight line ($49).
8Supplier sees the order in their portal. Site-access answers and delivery address are visible.

10.4 Dispatch flow (default - no claim)

9Supplier sets dispatch date → buyer gets email. Sets ETA → buyer gets email. Adds tracking URL → buyer gets email.
10Supplier moves status to In Transit. Order shows supplier_freight_cost = $49 (matches the quote). Activity log shows the change.
11Admin opens Xero, marks the PO as billed, accepts the draft Bill as-is, approves and pays. Returns to CQVS reconciliation, clicks Mark as billed on the row.

10.5 Variance flow (the contract exception)

12Place a fresh order. Supplier raises a variance claim for $75 with notification timestamp + evidence. Sees the pending claim on the order page.
13Admin opens the variance claims queue, sees the pending claim with full context. Approves at $65 (partial). Adds a decision note.
14Supplier and admin both receive approval emails. Supplier email says "you can invoice the approved amount". Admin email says "bump the Bill freight to $65 in Xero".
15Supplier moves the order to In Transit. supplier_freight_cost is now $65 (not $49 - the claim wins).
16Admin opens reconciliation in CQVS. The row is highlighted amber. Bill freight column says $65. Admin opens Xero, marks PO as billed, edits the draft Bill freight line from $49 to $65, approves, pays. Returns to CQVS, clicks Mark as billed. Highlight clears, row moves to "Billed in Xero" tab.

10.6 Window enforcement

17On an order already in In Transit, the supplier sees: "The claim window has closed (order is no longer pre-dispatch). You will be paid the originally quoted freight amount." The Raise variance claim button is hidden.

10.7 Locked snapshot

18Edit a bracket rate (e.g. raise it). Place a new order - new order quotes the new rate. The order from Step 5 still shows the old rate in its freight breakdown - the change is not retroactive.
Bottom line: the supplier owns their freight matrix, freight is locked when the buyer pays, the supplier is paid exactly the quote unless they raise an admin-approved variance claim, and the Bill (not the PO) is where any approved variance is reflected. Every change is audited, every actor is notified, and the reconciliation page tells admin exactly what to do in Xero.
