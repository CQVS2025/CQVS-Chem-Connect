Customer Reviews — SEO Feature Recommendation

A companion proposal to the Chem Connect SEO Optimisation Plan

About this document: This is a focused proposal for one specific feature — collecting and displaying genuine customer reviews on Chem Connect product pages. It’s a strong addition to the broader SEO plan, and it sits at the intersection of SEO, conversion, and buyer trust. Treat this as a recommendation, not a commitment — it can ship alongside, after, or independently of the rest of the SEO work.

1. What this is, in one sentence

A built-in flow that asks every customer to rate the product they bought after it’s delivered, captures the response with a one-click magic link, runs it through a quick admin moderation step, and publishes approved reviews on the product page — where they immediately feed Chem Connect’s SEO and conversion engines.

The infrastructure (the schema, the UI block, the rich-result markup) is already in the codebase from the SEO Optimisation Plan implementation. The piece this proposal adds is the collection mechanism — the bit that turns the empty “No reviews yet” placeholder into a stream of genuine, verified buyer feedback.

2. Why this matters for Chem Connect’s SEO

Reviews aren’t a vanity feature. On a B2B catalogue site they touch four distinct levers at once.

Lever 1 — Star ratings in Google search results (rich results)

Google rewards pages that have the right structured data and real reviews to back it. Without reviews, the product page renders as a plain blue link. With reviews, it renders with star ratings, review count, and price — taking up roughly twice the screen space of a plain result.

Why this lever matters
  • Industry data shows pages with rich star results get 20–35% more clicks at the same Google ranking position.
  • Same SEO position, more clicks — equivalent to ranking 1–2 spots higher without doing extra ranking work.
  • Visual differentiation against competitors who haven’t built this.

Lever 2 — Citations from AI search engines

ChatGPT, Perplexity, Microsoft Copilot, Claude, and Google AI Overviews all increasingly answer commercial queries (“where can I buy {chemical} in Australia?”) by surfacing and citing trusted product pages. These systems disproportionately favour pages with real reviews — reviews are a strong “this is a real, used product, not just a brochure” signal.

Building this gives Chem Connect a meaningful edge in the channel that’s growing fastest in B2B research.

Lever 3 — Closing the trust gap on first-time visits

B2B chemical buyers are sceptical by trade. A first-time visitor lands on the site with three open questions in their head:

  • “Is this an actual marketplace or a thin reseller?”
  • “Why is the pricing this much sharper than my current supplier?”
  • “Will the freight actually arrive when they say it will?”

A handful of named verified-buyer reviews from their own state — “Marcus T., Brisbane — verified buyer” — closes all three before they have to pick up the phone. It’s the on-page social proof that turns a curious visitor into an enquiry.

Lever 4 — A defensible moat against new entrants

Reviews accumulate on Chem Connect’s domain over time. A competitor entering the market in 12 or 24 months can copy the catalogue, the pricing, the design — but can’t copy the existing review base. Every month of real reviews collected widens the gap.

The combined effect: reviews don’t just help one ranking factor — they reinforce four at once. Star ratings lift CTR, AI search engines cite the page, first-time visitors convert at higher rates, and the moat compounds. There’s no single bigger return for the engineering effort involved.

3. How the collection flow will work

Three components, each chosen to keep friction off the buyer’s path while keeping the data clean and policy-compliant.

The flow at a glance

   an order
    |
    | (waits ~7 days — long enough for them to actually use it)
    v
Mailgun sends a personalised “How was the {product}?” email
    |
    | (one-click magic link — no login needed)
    v
…/reviews/submit?token=XYZ — review form, prefilled
    |
    | (status: pending)
    v
Admin moderation queue at /admin/marketing/reviews
    |
    | Approve → publishes on product page
    | Reject → stays in DB for audit, no display
    v

    + AggregateRating fed into Google rich result schema

Component A — The trigger fires after delivery, not at checkout

Asking for a review the moment someone places an order is annoying and pointless — they haven’t used the product yet. The review request fires when the order is marked delivered, with a deliberate delay so the buyer’s actually had time to put the product to work.

The trigger plugs into the existing GHL workflow / scheduled-campaign system already built for Chem Connect — same plumbing as the rest of the marketing module. No new infrastructure needed.

Component B — One-click magic links remove all friction

The email contains a personalised link with a cryptographically signed token. When the buyer clicks, they land on a pre-filled review form — their name, the product they bought, and the order ID are already populated. They write a star rating, a short review, hit submit. No login required.

B2B procurement people will not create an account just to leave a review. Magic links are how every modern B2B platform solves this exact problem — Stripe, Linear, Notion, etc. all use the same pattern.

Each token is one-use and expires after a fixed window, which prevents abuse if the email is forwarded or leaks.

Component C — Moderation before publication

Submitted reviews land in a pending queue inside the admin dashboard. Admin sees:

  • The reviewer’s name + verified-buyer badge
  • Their rating and review text
  • Which product / order it’s tied to
  • A clear Approve / Reject action

Approved reviews go live on the product page within seconds. Rejected reviews stay in the database for audit purposes (with a rejection reason recorded) but never display to shoppers.

4. What the buyer sees on the product page

Once approved reviews exist, the product page picks them up automatically. Three things appear:

Aggregate rating

Star average and total review count beside the product title. Visible on the page and in Google’s search results via the existing AggregateRating schema.

Individual review cards

Each approved review with star rating, headline, body, reviewer name + city + state, date, and a “Verified buyer” badge.

Empty-state graceful fallback

If a product has no reviews yet, the section says “No reviews yet — be the first”. No broken UI, no fake placeholder.

Verified-buyer badging

Because the magic link includes the order ID, every review is automatically tagged as Verified buyer on submission. That’s a meaningful trust signal — a lot of review platforms (e.g. ProductReview.com.au) require manual verification, which most reviewers can’t be bothered with. Doing it through the email loop makes verification automatic.

5. The compliance approach

Reviews are a regulated area in Australia. The flow is designed to comply with both Google’s policy and the Australian Consumer Law from day one.

RuleHow this implementation handles it
Reviews must be from genuine buyers (Google policy + AU Consumer Law) Magic-link tokens are tied to real order IDs. A review can’t be submitted without a valid token, and tokens can’t be forged.
No editing reviews to make them more positive (s18, AU Consumer Law) Admin can only approve or reject — no edit capability in the moderation UI. What the buyer wrote is what gets published.
No “review gating” (asking only happy customers) Every delivered order triggers a review request. We don’t pre-screen for satisfaction.
Negative reviews must not be hidden Rejection is allowed only for clear policy violations (PII, libel, off-topic, obvious fakes), not for low ratings.
Audit trail for moderation decisions Every approve / reject action records the moderator, timestamp, and (for rejections) reason — queryable if ever needed.
Reply to reviews publicly Admin can post a reply to any approved review — same way professional shops handle Google reviews. Builds trust further.
What this implementation will not do:
  • No Display fake / seed reviews to real shoppers (that’s a Google penalty trigger and an ACCC misleading-conduct risk).
  • No Edit review text to soften criticism.
  • No Hide low-rating reviews from the page or the schema.
  • No Solicit reviews in exchange for discounts (incentivised reviews must be disclosed under AU law — cleaner to skip the practice entirely).

6. The Schema.org wiring (already in place)

The product detail page already emits a Schema.org Product JSON-LD block. Once reviews exist for a product, the implementation splices two extra fields into that block:

AggregateRating

Drives the star result in Google search. Includes the average rating, review count, and best/worst rating bounds.

Review[] array

Up to ten individual reviews per product, each with author name, rating, date, and review body. Rounds out the rich-result eligibility.

Both schemas validate against Google’s Rich Results Test and the Schema.org validator. They’re also picked up by ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews when those systems crawl the page.

8. Recommendation

Build it — alongside the rest of the SEO Optimisation Plan. It’s the single highest-leverage SEO addition that isn’t already in scope, and the existing infrastructure (schema, UI block, AggregateRating wiring) means most of the work is glue rather than greenfield.

There’s no version of Chem Connect at scale where customer reviews aren’t one of the most-touched parts of the platform — SEO, conversion, AI search, brand, all reinforce each other through this one feature. The longer the collection flow runs, the larger the moat becomes.

Default position if not built: the existing “No reviews yet — be the first” placeholder remains visible, the AggregateRating schema stays disabled, and the product pages render as plain blue links in search results. Nothing breaks — but the rich-result, AI-citation, and trust upsides described above don’t materialise.
